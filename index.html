Understanding Your IP Address

Every device that connects to the internet is assigned an IP address, a numeric label that lets networks route data to and from it correctly. Without this address, a website would have no way of knowing where to send the page you requested. Think of it as a postal code for your connection: not precise enough to find your front door, but accurate enough to get the data to roughly the right neighborhood.

What Is an IPv4 Address

IPv4 is the original addressing system still used by most home and mobile connections today. It writes an address as four numbers between 0 and 255, separated by dots, such as 192.168.1.1. This format allows for a little over four billion unique addresses, a number that seemed enormous in the 1980s but has since become a real constraint as billions of new devices came online.

What Is an IPv6 Address

IPv6 was designed to solve that shortage. It uses a much longer 128-bit format written in hexadecimal groups separated by colons, which expands the available address space to a number large enough to assign a unique address to every grain of sand on Earth many times over. Adoption has been gradual, and many networks now run IPv4 and IPv6 side by side.

Public vs Private IP Addresses

A private IP address is only visible inside your home or office network — your router, laptop, and phone each get one so they can talk to each other. A public IP address is the one visible to the rest of the internet, usually assigned to your router by your provider and shared across every device behind it through a process called network address translation.

Why IP Location Isn't Always Accurate

IP geolocation databases estimate location by matching address ranges to records reported by internet providers, not by tracking a device directly. That means accuracy is usually reliable at the country level, often correct at the city level, and rarely exact enough to pinpoint a building.

Static vs Dynamic IP Addresses

A static IP address stays the same indefinitely and is common for servers and business connections that need a fixed, predictable address. A dynamic IP address is reassigned periodically by the provider, which is standard for most home internet plans and is one reason your address may look different from one day to the next.

How to Protect Your Privacy Online

Because your IP address is visible to every site you visit, some people choose to mask it for privacy, security, or to access region-restricted content.

Using a VPN

A virtual private network routes your traffic through a remote server and encrypts it along the way, so websites see the VPN server's IP address instead of your own. This is the most common and generally most reliable method for hiding your real address while browsing.

Using Proxies

A proxy server also relays your traffic through a different address, but typically without the encryption a VPN provides. Proxies are often faster for simple tasks but offer weaker protection against anyone monitoring the connection itself.

Tor vs VPN

Tor routes traffic through several volunteer-run relays and layers of encryption, making it harder to trace but noticeably slower than a typical VPN. A VPN usually offers better speed for everyday use, while Tor is built for stronger anonymity when speed is a lower priority.

IP Address Glossary

Networking Terms

ISP

An internet service provider is the company that connects your home, office, or mobile device to the internet and assigns the public IP address your connection uses.

Tier 1 vs Tier 2 ISPs

Tier 1 providers own long-distance backbone infrastructure and exchange traffic directly with each other, while Tier 2 providers typically pay for some of their connectivity through a Tier 1 network.

Example

A regional cable or fiber provider serving one city is usually a Tier 2 or Tier 3 ISP, purchasing upstream bandwidth from a larger backbone operator.

ASN

An autonomous system number identifies a network operator on the internet, such as an ISP or large company, and is used in routing decisions between networks.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is my IP address?

Your IP address is the unique numeric identifier assigned to your device by your internet service provider so it can send and receive data online. This tool detects and displays it automatically at the top of the page.

Is my IP address my exact location?

No. Your IP address maps to an approximate location, usually your city or region, based on records held by your internet provider. It is not your exact street address and accuracy varies by provider and connection type.

Can someone track me using my IP address?

An IP address alone reveals only your approximate region and internet provider, not your identity or precise home address. Combined with other data it can contribute to tracking, which is why a VPN adds privacy.

Why does my IP address change?

Most home internet connections use dynamic IP addresses that your provider reassigns periodically or when you restart your router. Mobile networks and VPNs also change your visible IP whenever you switch networks or servers.

What is the difference between IPv4 and IPv6?

IPv4 uses a 32-bit address written as four numbers separated by dots, giving about 4.3 billion possible addresses, which is now nearly exhausted. IPv6 uses a 128-bit address written in hexadecimal groups, providing a vastly larger supply for the growing number of connected devices.
