What Is an IP Address?

An IP address (Internet Protocol address) is a unique numerical label assigned to every device connected to a computer network that uses the Internet Protocol for communication. Think of it as the digital equivalent of a postal address: it tells the internet where to send data so it reaches the right destination.

Without an IP address, your computer, phone, or smart TV could not send or receive information online. Every website you visit, every email you send, and every video you stream relies on IP addresses to route data between your device and servers around the world.

There are two main versions of IP addresses in use today: IPv4 and IPv6. IPv4 has been the standard since the early days of the internet, but with billions of new devices coming online, the world is gradually transitioning to IPv6, which offers a virtually unlimited number of addresses.

IPv4 vs IPv6: What's the Difference?

IPv4 addresses look like 192.168.1.1 or 8.8.8.8. They are 32-bit numbers written as four groups of digits (0–255) separated by dots. IPv4 supports approximately 4.3 billion unique addresses — a number that seemed enormous in the 1980s but is now exhausted.

IPv6 addresses look like 2001:0db8:85a3:0000:0000:8a2e:0370:7334. They are 128-bit numbers written as eight groups of four hexadecimal digits separated by colons. IPv6 supports roughly 340 undecillion addresses — enough to assign billions of addresses to every grain of sand on Earth.

Most modern networks support both protocols simultaneously through a technique called dual-stack, where devices can communicate over IPv4 or IPv6 depending on what the destination supports.

Public vs Private IP Address

A public IP address is the address assigned to your home or office network by your Internet Service Provider (ISP). It is visible to the entire internet and is what websites, online games, and streaming services see when you connect to them. If you look up "what is my IP" on this page, the result is your public IP.

A private IP address is used only inside your local network — between your router, laptop, phone, printer, and smart home devices. Private IPs are not reachable from the internet. Common private IP ranges include:

  • 192.168.0.0 to 192.168.255.255
  • 10.0.0.0 to 10.255.255.255
  • 172.16.0.0 to 172.31.255.255

Your router uses a technology called NAT (Network Address Translation) to share one public IP among all your devices, each with its own private IP. This is why every device in your home can browse the web simultaneously using a single public IP.

How to Find Your IP Address

Finding your IP address is simple, but the method depends on whether you need your public IP (visible to the internet) or your private IP (used inside your local network). Below are step-by-step instructions for every major device and operating system.

Method 1: Use an Online IP Lookup Tool (Fastest)

  1. Open any web browser on the device you want to check.
  2. Visit this page or any IP lookup website.
  3. Your public IP address is displayed automatically at the top of the page.
  4. You will also see your approximate location, ISP, timezone, and ASN.

Tip: This is the easiest way to find your public IP because it works on any device with a browser — Windows, Mac, Linux, iPhone, Android, or even a smart TV.

Method 2: Find Your IP on Windows

For your private IP:

  1. Press Win + R, type cmd, and press Enter to open Command Prompt.
  2. Type ipconfig and press Enter.
  3. Look for the "IPv4 Address" line under your active network adapter (Wi-Fi or Ethernet).
  4. The number shown is your private IP address, for example 192.168.1.105.

For your public IP: Open a browser and visit an IP lookup site like this one. Windows does not show your public IP in system settings.

Method 3: Find Your IP on Mac

For your private IP:

  1. Click the Apple menu and select System Settings (or System Preferences on older versions).
  2. Click Network in the sidebar.
  3. Select your active connection (Wi-Fi or Ethernet).
  4. Your IP address is displayed in the connection details.

For your public IP: Open Safari or any browser and visit an IP lookup website.

Method 4: Find Your IP on iPhone or Android

iPhone (private IP):

  1. Open Settings and tap Wi-Fi.
  2. Tap the info icon (ⓘ) next to your connected network.
  3. Look for the "IP Address" field.

Android (private IP):

  1. Open Settings and go to Network & Internet (or Connections on Samsung).
  2. Tap Wi-Fi, then tap your connected network.
  3. Your IP address is listed under network details.

For your public IP on mobile: Open any browser and search "what is my ip" or visit this page.

Method 5: Find Your IP on Your Router

  1. Open a browser and enter your router's admin address, usually 192.168.1.1 or 192.168.0.1.
  2. Log in with your admin username and password (often printed on a sticker on the router).
  3. Navigate to the Status, WAN, or Internet section.
  4. Your public IP address is displayed there, along with your connection type and uptime.

Why IP Location Isn't Exact

When you look up an IP address, the location shown is an estimate based on your ISP's registered data, not GPS. IP geolocation databases map IP ranges to physical addresses using registration records from Regional Internet Registries (RIRs) like ARIN, RIPE, and APNIC.

At the country level, IP geolocation is highly accurate — usually over 95% correct. At the city level, accuracy drops to roughly 50–80% depending on the region and ISP. It is never precise enough to pinpoint a street address or a specific building. If you see a location that is a neighboring city, that is completely normal.

Several factors affect IP location accuracy:

  • ISP headquarters: Some ISPs register all their IPs to their corporate office, which may be hundreds of miles from your actual home.
  • Mobile networks: Cellular IPs often appear to come from a major city where the carrier's data center is located, not your current GPS position.
  • VPNs and proxies: If you use a VPN, the location shown will be the VPN server's location, not yours.
  • Dynamic IPs: When your IP changes, the geolocation database may take days or weeks to update.

Static vs Dynamic IP Address

A static IP address never changes. It is manually configured and typically used by businesses, servers, and remote-access systems that need a consistent address. Static IPs usually cost extra from your ISP and are less common for residential customers.

A dynamic IP address changes periodically. Most home internet plans use dynamic IPs because they are more efficient for ISPs. Your IP may change when you restart your router, after a power outage, or simply because your ISP rotates addresses among customers. Dynamic IPs are perfectly fine for everyday browsing, streaming, and gaming.

How to Hide Your IP Address

Your public IP address is visible to every website, app, and online service you use. While it does not reveal your exact identity, it can expose your approximate location, your ISP, and your browsing patterns over time. If you value privacy, here are the three most common ways to hide your real IP.

VPN (Virtual Private Network)

A VPN is the most popular and user-friendly way to hide your IP. It creates an encrypted tunnel between your device and a remote server operated by the VPN provider. All your internet traffic passes through this tunnel, and websites see the VPN server's IP instead of yours.

Pros: Strong encryption, easy to use, works on all devices, hides traffic from your ISP, bypasses geo-restrictions.

Cons: Can slow down your connection, requires trust in the VPN provider, premium services cost money (free VPNs often sell data).

Proxy Server

A proxy acts as an intermediary between you and the internet. Your traffic goes to the proxy first, and the proxy forwards it to the destination using its own IP. Unlike a VPN, most proxies do not encrypt your traffic.

Pros: Often free, can be faster than VPNs, useful for bypassing simple geo-blocks.

Cons: No encryption (your ISP can still see what you do), less secure, some proxies log your activity, not all apps support proxy settings.

Tor (The Onion Router)

Tor is a free, open-source network that bounces your traffic through three random volunteer-run relays before reaching its destination. Each relay only knows the previous and next hop, making it extremely difficult to trace traffic back to you.

Pros: Highest level of anonymity, free, open source, resistant to surveillance.

Cons: Much slower than VPNs or regular browsing, some websites block Tor exit nodes, not suitable for streaming or large downloads.

What Can Someone Do With Your IP?

Your IP address alone is not enough for someone to steal your identity or hack your bank account. However, it does reveal enough information to enable certain risks. Understanding these risks helps you decide whether hiding your IP is worth it for your situation.

  • Approximate geolocation: Anyone with your IP can estimate your city, region, and country. This is how websites show localized content and ads.
  • ISP identification: Your IP reveals which company provides your internet service. This is usually harmless but can be combined with other data for profiling.
  • DDoS attacks: In online gaming or competitive environments, malicious actors can target your IP with a Distributed Denial of Service attack, overwhelming your connection and knocking you offline.
  • Port scanning: Attackers can scan your public IP for open ports and vulnerable services. A well-configured router firewall blocks most of these attempts.
  • Tracking across sites: Advertising networks can use your IP (along with cookies and browser fingerprinting) to build a profile of your browsing habits over time.

The simplest way to eliminate most of these risks is to use a reputable VPN, which replaces your real IP with one shared by thousands of other users.

IP Address Glossary

Understanding IP-related terminology helps you make informed decisions about your network, privacy, and security. Here are the most important terms explained in plain English.

ISP (Internet Service Provider)

The company that provides your internet connection and assigns your public IP address. Examples include Comcast, AT&T, Verizon, BT, and Deutsche Telekom. ISPs are classified into tiers based on their network infrastructure:

  • Tier 1 ISP: Owns physical fiber-optic cables and infrastructure across continents. Can reach every other network on the internet without paying for transit. Examples: Level 3, Cogent, Tata Communications.
  • Tier 2 ISP: Has a large network but pays Tier 1 providers for access to parts of the internet it does not own. Most residential and business ISPs fall into this category.
  • Tier 3 ISP: Purchases all internet access from Tier 2 providers. Often local or regional cable companies and smaller ISPs.

ASN (Autonomous System Number)

An ASN is a unique number assigned to a network operator (like an ISP, university, or large corporation) that identifies their collection of IP addresses and routing policies on the internet. ASNs are used by the Border Gateway Protocol (BGP) to exchange routing information between networks. When you look up an IP, the ASN tells you which organization controls the network block that IP belongs to.

DNS (Domain Name System)

DNS is the phonebook of the internet. When you type google.com into your browser, DNS translates that human-readable domain name into an IP address (like 142.250.80.46) so your device knows where to send the request. Without DNS, you would have to memorize numerical IP addresses for every website.

NAT (Network Address Translation)

NAT is the technology your router uses to allow multiple devices on your home network to share a single public IP address. It maps each device's private IP and port number to the router's public IP, ensuring responses from the internet reach the correct device.

Subnet Mask

A subnet mask defines the boundaries of your local network. It tells your device which IP addresses are on the same local network (reachable directly) and which are outside (requiring a router). A common home subnet mask is 255.255.255.0, meaning the first three parts of the IP identify the network and the last part identifies the device.

Default Gateway

The default gateway is the IP address of your router on your local network. It is the "door" through which all traffic destined for the internet passes. On most home networks, the default gateway is 192.168.1.1 or 192.168.0.1.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is my IP address?

Your IP address is the number your internet provider gives your device so it can send and receive data online. It's shown automatically at the top of this page.

Is my IP address my exact location?

No. It points to your rough city or region, not your street address, and accuracy varies by provider. IP geolocation is based on ISP registration data, not GPS.

Can someone track me using my IP address?

By itself, an IP only shows your rough region and provider, not your identity. However, it can be combined with other data for tracking. A VPN can hide it for extra privacy.

Why does my IP address change?

Most home connections use dynamic IPs that your provider reassigns from time to time, including after a router restart. Business plans sometimes offer static IPs for an extra fee.

IPv4 vs IPv6 — what's the difference?

IPv4 addresses are shorter (like 192.168.1.1) and nearly used up. IPv6 is a longer format (like 2001:0db8::1) built to support the billions of new devices connecting to the internet.

What is a public IP address?

A public IP is the address assigned to your network by your ISP. It is visible to the entire internet and is what websites see when you visit them. This page displays your public IP automatically.

What is a private IP address?

A private IP is used only inside your home or office network. It is assigned by your router and is not visible from the internet. Common private IPs look like 192.168.1.x or 10.0.0.x.

How do I hide my IP address?

Use a VPN, proxy server, or the Tor network. A VPN is the easiest and most secure option for everyday use. It encrypts your traffic and replaces your real IP with the VPN server's IP.

What can someone do with my IP address?

They can estimate your general location, identify your ISP, and potentially launch DDoS or port-scanning attacks. They cannot see your exact address, name, or browsing history from the IP alone.
